# The Terminal UI Frameworks Field Report, 2026

**Date: 2026-07-06**

*Methodology: this piece is synthesized from a structured survey of thirteen framework studies — Textual, Ink, Bubble Tea, Ratatui, Cursive, Brick, libvaxis/vxfw, OpenTUI, Codex's tui2, the Rust-GUI literature, the terminal substrate layer, the pre-2024 "next-gen Rust TUI" attempts, and the 2024–2026 framework wave. Every load-bearing claim is sourced to a public issue, discussion, commit, blog post, or spec; URLs are inline. No vendor briefings, no marketing decks. Where a number appears, it was counted.*

---

## The field, in one paragraph

Terminal user interfaces are having their loudest decade since curses, and the noise is almost entirely coming from Rust. In the twenty-four months to mid-2026, more than fifty new Rust TUI frameworks were published — a companion sweep counts sixty-three — and roughly a third of them are substantially AI-generated. That sounds like a Cambrian explosion of competing designs. It is not. Read the fifty of them side by side and they converge on one feature list — event loop, focus, forms, overlays, theming, an MVU-or-hooks programming model, and inline (scrollback-preserving) rendering — so tightly that the wave is best understood not as a competition but as *the largest demand survey ever run against the gap Ratatui deliberately left open*. Meanwhile the mature frameworks in every other language spent the same two years quietly conceding the same architectural points, and the coding-agent CLIs that now define the flagship workload burned through a year of renderer engineering to relearn lessons that buffer-diffing TUI libraries have known since ncurses. The interesting story of 2026 is not who is winning. It is what everyone, independently, was forced to learn.

---

## Part I: The wave is a survey, not a race

Start with the vibe-coded frameworks, because they are the most misread datum in the field. A representative sample from the 2025–2026 cohort: `ratada` (a "driver, modals, forms, pickers, theming" crate published on day one, 19 downloads), `weavetui` (`#[component]` macros plus a keybinding DSL), `textual-rs` (a Textual port with 963 commits in thirty days against a single GitHub star), `karet` (a fifteen-crate editor toolkit published all at once on 2026-07-02), `charmed_rust` (a whole-cloth port of Charm's Go ecosystem). The tells are unmistakable: multi-crate day-one publishes, download-to-star ratios that make no sense, `CLAUDE.md` and `AGENTS.md` engineering constitutions committed to the repo, and verbatim-identical crate descriptions — `tuyere` and `charmed-bubbletea` ship the same LLM boilerplate sentence.

The instinct is to dismiss these as noise. That is the mistake. An LLM writing a TUI framework is trained on the community's stated wants; a vibe-coded framework is therefore *a compressed replay of every "I wish Ratatui had X" thread ever posted*. And the striking result is that the AI-generated frameworks converge on exactly the same missing-middle feature list as the hand-built ones. The AI-generated "missing middle" crates (`ratada`, `weavetui`) name the identical gaps — event loop, focus, forms, overlays, theming — that the human-built `rat-salsa` systematically shipped starting in 2024. The convergence *is* the survey result: when a stochastic parrot and a diligent solo maintainer independently produce the same crate manifest, you have found the shape of real demand.

What does that demand look like? Ranked by how many independent projects express it:

| Rank | The want | Evidence in the wave |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | The "missing middle": event loop, focus, forms, overlays, theming | `rat-salsa`'s 14-crate 2024 build ≈ `ratada`'s 2026 day-one manifest; `ratatui-interact` did 22.6k downloads in six months on *just* focus + mouse |
| 2 | Batteries-included widget catalog, consistently versioned | "you take on third party dependencies for each individual widget… basic things like spinners, checkboxes, text areas" — ModernMech, [HN 45830829](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45830829) |
| 3 | Elm/MVU runtime (Bubble Tea envy) | ≥10 TEA ports in 18 months; `bubbletea-rs` and `hojicha` published the *same week* |
| 4 | React/Ink-style hooks + declarative components | three independent Ink clones in six months (`rnk`, `ntui`, `eye-declare`) |
| 5 | **Inline rendering that preserves scrollback** | `eye-declare`, `rnk` (inline-by-default), `FrankenTUI` — the sharpest *new* demand |
| 6 | Rendering perf/correctness as a feature | "React isn't the terminal UI bottleneck, the output pipeline is" — CellState, [HN 47442385](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=47442385) |
| 7 | Web-grade styling: three independent literal-CSS engines in 2026 | `textual-rs`, `ratatui-style`, `revue` |
| 8 | Async-first runtime with reactive state | waker-driven rendering (`ratatui-kit`); counter-demand: *don't mandate tokio* |

Two entries on that list are the ones to watch. Number seven — three separate people independently reimplemented CSS-with-cascade for the terminal in a single year — tells you that Textual's styling model is the single most envied feature in the field. And number five, inline rendering, is the sharpest *new* demand of the era, and it has a specific cause: AI coding-agent CLIs.

The AI agents are not just the flagship workload. They are also a major *author* population (that third of the wave), and they introduced a new design axis nobody had before: agent-legible APIs. `SuperLightTUI` pitches "a small public grammar… easily inferrable from documentation, benefiting both human developers and coding agents." `ratatui-kit` ships and evaluates an *agent skill for itself*. `testty` exists purely so agents can PTY-test TUIs they cannot see. This is genuinely new: for the first time, "can a language model write a correct app against this framework by reading the docs" is a competitive dimension.

The adoption reality underneath all this is brutal and clarifying. Layered-on-Ratatui frameworks win (`Ratzilla` at 1,401 stars, `rat-salsa`, `ratatui-interact`); from-scratch stacks cap out around 400 stars regardless of quality (`AppCUI-rs` at 396, `yeehaw` at 354, `Anathema` at 352). And the most complete architecture in the entire survey — `rooibos`, which already ships Leptos-style fine-grained signals, `Send + Sync` reactivity, Taffy layout, async-first, a testing-library-inspired test crate, and terminal/SSH/web/testing backends — has **five stars**. It self-describes as "pre-alpha… should not be used for anything beyond experimentation." That is the most important cautionary tale in the field: *the right feature list is worth nothing without docs, a widget catalog, a flagship app, and marketing.* The HN crowd, asked directly, does not request signals or reconcilers. They ask for "a widget library and an event loop that I like" (alfiedotwtf, [HN 45830829](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=45830829)). Architecture novelty is the cheapest thing in the field now. Fifty frameworks ship signals, hooks, TEA, ECS, and immediate-mode closures, and none of it moves adoption.

## Part II: The convergences — what everyone independently learned

Strip away the language wars and something remarkable emerges: frameworks built by different people, in different languages, for different eras, arriving at the same handful of conclusions without coordinating. These are the load-bearing findings of the survey, because a conclusion reached seven times independently is not an opinion.

### 1. Widget identity must be framework-owned, and it is the one problem that transfers in full

This is the deepest convergence in the field. Every mature framework needs a widget to be "the same widget" across frames — for focus, scroll persistence, cursor/IME position, event routing, and any future accessibility story — and *every single one arrived at framework-owned stable identity from a different starting point*:

- **Brick** (Haskell) makes a user-defined resource-name type `n` the key for viewport scroll offsets, render cache, click regions, extents, and focus — one namespace unifying everything ratatui users reinvent ad hoc. Duplicate names are a runtime `error` with an apology message.
- **Cursive** (Rust, retained) keys identity on *strings*: `NamedView` wraps a view with a `String` name, and `call_on_name` does an O(tree) walk with a triple downcast that returns `None` on either a wrong name *or* a wrong type, silently. Community code is littered with `.unwrap()` on it ([cursive #92 lineage](https://github.com/gyscos/Cursive/issues/92)).
- **libvaxis's vxfw** (Zig) keys identity on *raw pointer equality* (`userdata` ptr + `drawFn` ptr) — and [discussion #232](https://github.com/rockorager/libvaxis/discussions/232) is a user whose ephemeral `Text` widgets immediately dangle into freed stack frames. The community fix is "keep an ArrayList of widgets with stable pointers" — i.e., hand-implement the retained state the framework should own.
- **Xilem** (Rust GUI) settled on **id-paths** (`[1,3]` from the root) that triple as identity, event route, and async waker address ([Xilem architecture](https://raphlinus.github.io/rust/gui/2022/05/07/ui-architecture.html)).
- **Masonry** (Rust GUI) named its single biggest 2024 win as moving from parent-owned children to **arena/slotmap storage** where "widgets own keys into a shared structure," solving the borrow-checker knot of mutating a container's children while holding a live reference to it ([post-mortem](https://poignardazur.github.io/2025/03/24/plan-for-linebender-post-mortem/)).
- **Textual** (Python, retained) gets identity for free precisely *because* widgets are persistent objects: focus is "just a reference to a node that survives every repaint, which is precisely what immediate-mode frameworks have to fake with IDs and hashing."
- **Bubble Tea** (Go) has *no* answer, and its tracker is the negative control. [Discussion #751](https://github.com/charmbracelet/bubbletea/discussions/751) — "no way to know which child a reply message belongs to" — spawns hand-rolled `Wrap()` integer-tagging schemes; [#707](https://github.com/charmbracelet/bubbletea/discussions/707), on sharing state between sibling components, got **zero replies**. The framework simply has no notion of which component owns keyboard input, so every serious app rebuilds a `focusIndex int` traversal by hand off the official `textinputs` example.

Seven frameworks, seven starting points, one destination. The Rust-GUI literature states the underlying law plainly: pure immediate mode "fundamentally struggles" with identity, and accessibility APIs, which assume a retained stably-identified tree, "eliminate pure immediate mode as viable for production" ([Next dozen GUIs](https://raphlinus.github.io/rust/gui/2022/07/15/next-dozen-guis.html)). Identity is the problem that transfers to terminals *undiminished*, because it is a reactivity problem, not a pixel problem.

### 2. String diffing is a dead end; the cell grid won

For six years Bubble Tea's `View()` returned one big string, and its renderer diffed line-by-line at ~60fps. In February 2026 — its **first breaking change in six years** — v2 changed `View()` from a `string` to a declarative `tea.View` struct and rebuilt rendering on a real cell grid "based on the ncurses rendering algorithm," claiming order-of-magnitude gains over SSH ([v2 announcement](https://charm.land/blog/v2/)). Its own team conceded string diffing as the dead end it was.

Ink tells the same story from the other side. Its architecture — React render → full Yoga layout → paint every cell into a grid → serialize to one big string → diff *at the string stage* — puts all the cost before the diff, and the result is five years of flicker bugs, headlined by [#359](https://github.com/vadimdemedes/ink/issues/359), open since 2020: content taller than the viewport "flickers badly on updates," structurally unfixable because `eraseLines` can only erase what is still on screen. OpenTUI's creator, explaining why OpenCode left Bubble Tea, put the general principle bluntly: Bubble Tea "operates on strings so that makes a lot of things difficult — like performance and text detection" ([thdxr](https://x.com/thdxr/status/1959934254126669998)). The whole field has now landed on: render into a cell buffer, diff two buffers, emit only changed cells. Ratatui shipped that in 2016; Bubble Tea shipped it in 2026.

### 3. The Ink / Claude Code renderer saga, and the punchline

No episode compresses the era's lessons better than what happened when the JavaScript agent CLIs hit Ink's rendering limits at industrial scale.

Anthropic's Claude Code spent roughly a year on renderer surgery: it replaced Ink's renderer with a custom one (~v2.0.10), added cell-level differential rendering (~v2.0.72), used packed TypedArrays to dodge GC-pause artifacts, and pushed DEC mode 2026 (synchronized output) patches *upstream* into VS Code's terminal and tmux ([steipete.me](https://steipete.me/posts/2025/signature-flicker), [slyapustin.com](https://slyapustin.com/blog/claude-code-no-flicker.html)). Google shipped Ink to millions in Gemini CLI and accumulated a flicker bug tax — [#8387](https://github.com/google-gemini/gemini-cli/issues/8387) ("causing headache and discomfort"), a whole rendering epic ([#10673](https://github.com/google-gemini/gemini-cli/issues/10673)) asking for an alt-screen toggle and "a performant alternative for Ink static."

The punchline: the fix that actually worked was `CLAUDE_CODE_NO_FLICKER=1` — the **alternate-screen buffer, "like every other TUI."** A year of custom renderers, TypedArray GC engineering, and upstream terminal patches to approximate what a buffer-diffing TUI library does natively out of the box.

And the second punchline, easy to miss: Codex CLI left Node entirely for the *runtime*, not the renderer. The maintainer-stated reasons for the Rust rewrite ([discussion #1174](https://github.com/openai/codex/discussions/1174)) were a zero-dependency install ("Node v22+ is required, which is frustrating or a blocker"), native sandboxing bindings already in Rust, "no runtime garbage collection, resulting in lower memory consumption," and a language-agnostic wire protocol. Notably, when Anthropic ripped out Ink's renderer, they *kept React as the component model* — the DX layer was worth preserving; only the paint layer was broken. The programming model won even as the substrate lost.

### 4. Elm subscriptions were deleted and never missed

Bubble Tea *had* Elm-style subscriptions (`tea.Sub`, `tea.Every`) in early 2020 and deleted them in commit `ade8203c`: "we can achieve the same functionality in a much simpler fashion with commands." A recurring timer becomes a command that re-issues itself; a long-lived stream becomes a command blocking on a channel. Six years later nobody has asked for subscriptions back. This is a rare clean subtraction in UI architecture: two concepts (commands *and* subscriptions) collapsed to one (commands) with no loss of power — a result worth remembering the next time someone proposes a second effect primitive.

### 5. The modest layout vocabulary is enough; nobody missed the constraint solver

Textual's entire shipped ecosystem — Harlequin (a SQL IDE), Posting (an HTTP client), Toolong (a log viewer) — runs on a layout vocabulary that is *neither flexbox nor a constraint solver*: dock (pin to an edge, remove from flow) plus linear plus grid plus fractional `fr` units, with the fractions computed in exact `Fraction` arithmetic so `1fr 1fr 1fr` never leaves a one-cell gap. "Nobody missed flexbox wrap or cassowary." Ink reached the same finding from flexbox's side: five years of its issue tracker contain layout-*semantics* complaints approaching zero; all the pain is in the paint layer. Cursive converged too — two-phase `required_size`/`layout` negotiation, no solver, "Cursive never missed it." Ratatui, which inherited cassowary, is the outlier, and even there the story is retreat: `cassowary-rs` has been unmaintained since 2018 and the project forked it into `kasuari` because the upstream "inconsistent expansion, overconstraint panics" became untenable ([RFC #1933](https://github.com/ratatui/ratatui/discussions/1933)). The verified lesson is: a constraint solver is over-general for the terminal; text measurement folded into layout is the thing that is actually missing.

### 6. Damage regions are worthless on a cell grid — the diff *is* the damage tracking

This is the cleanest transfer-analysis result from the GUI literature, and it cuts the other way from what desktop instincts suggest. A GUI framework spends enormous effort on damage regions, repaint boundaries, and compositor relationships because rasterization and GPU upload are expensive. A terminal frame is at most ~100k cells; diffing two buffers and emitting escape sequences is microseconds. Therefore **damage regions are unnecessary — the double-buffer diff is the damage tracking, computed for free after the fact** ([rust-gui-lessons synthesis](https://raphlinus.github.io/rust/druid/2020/09/28/rust-2021.html)). Cursive proves this the hard way: it is a *retained* tree, and people assumed retention granted partial redraw — but it re-layouts and re-draws the whole tree every frame and diffs a `PrintBuffer` at the end, and [issue #667](https://github.com/gyscos/cursive/issues/667) (a frame costing 174 KB over SSH vs ncurses' 11 KB) existed *because* the retained tree gave no partial redraw at all. The supposed advantage of retention — incremental paint — is simply absent. The complexity budget a terminal framework saves by *not* building damage tracking should be spent upstream, on skipping view and layout work, which is where the only real cost lives.

The corollary the same analysis delivers: the thing that actually kills pure immediate mode is not performance. **It is accessibility.** Cheap redraws mean immediate mode's paint cost never bites. What bites is that screen-reader APIs assume a retained, stably-identified tree — and in a fifty-framework wave, *nobody* addressed assistive-technology semantics. That silence is the field's largest open problem, discussed below.

## Part III: The instructive failures

Convergences tell you where the field agrees. The negative results tell you what it costs to disagree — and two experiments in this survey are worth more than any success story.

### Codex tui2: the era's sharpest negative result

Codex CLI's `tui2` was a production coding-agent TUI (Ratatui, Rust) that rearchitected its viewport so that *the app, not the terminal, owned scrollback, selection, and copy*. The motivation was real: `tui1` cooperates with the terminal's native scrollback (writing finalized history above an inline viewport via raw scroll-region escapes), which means it inherits every emulator's idiosyncrasies — "what looked correct in one terminal could drop or duplicate content in another," and resize/reflow corrupted history because it was printed pre-wrapped at the width-of-the-moment.

So tui2 made the in-memory transcript the single source of truth: a flat `Vec<Arc<dyn HistoryCell>>` where each cell stores **width-agnostic content** and wraps at render time, addressed by stable cell index. It won decisively on the two things it targeted. Resize-rewrap became correct. Copy fidelity became excellent — selection lives in content-relative coordinates that track the text as it scrolls, and copy reconstructs off-screen ranges, joins soft-wrapped prose, and emits *markdown source* even where the UI rendered it away.

And then it was **retired**. The retirement commit ([#9640](https://github.com/openai/codex/commit/a489b64cb5)) is the honest verdict: *"What worked: a transcript-owned viewport delivered excellent resize rewrap and high-fidelity copy… Why stop: making that experience feel fully native across the environment matrix (terminal emulator, OS, input modality, multiplexer, font/theme, alt-screen behavior) creates a combinatorial explosion of edge cases."* The user constituency it provoked had already filed [issue #8344](https://github.com/openai/codex/issues/8344), "Don't mess with the native TUI": *"Terminal is king because anything works anywhere. Don't break scrolling, copy/paste, for crying out loud."*

The lesson is not "own the viewport" or "don't." It is that owning the viewport is a *total commitment* that trades a hard rendering problem for a harder terminal-compatibility problem — and that a CLI shipping to every terminal on earth will be punished for the second one. The cost is concrete and under-budgeted: tui2's scroll model is documented in a study of **13,734 scroll events across 8 terminals**, whose central finding is that terminals emit 1–9+ raw events per physical wheel notch and **timing cannot distinguish a wheel from a trackpad**. Owning the viewport means *reimplementing the wheel* — per-terminal events-per-tick normalization (AppleTerminal 3, Warp 9, WezTerm 1…), stream grouping, a wheel-vs-trackpad heuristic, and a mandatory user override because the heuristic is best-effort. That is what "app-owned scrollback" actually costs.

tui2 did leave two artifacts every framework author should steal. First, its `Renderable::desired_height(width)` measurement hook plus Flutter-style column/flex composition — the exact content-measurement primitive Ratatui's 1-D constraint layout structurally lacks ([discussion #552](https://github.com/ratatui/ratatui/discussions/552)) — a production app reinventing a slice of proper layout. Second, its rule of thumb, which is a correctness requirement for any streaming-markdown workload: *any cell that stores pre-wrapped `Vec<Line>` for reflowable prose is a reflow bug waiting to happen; store source/logical lines and wrap inside `display_lines(width)`.* And the soft-wrap-vs-hard-break distinction must be *data* (tui2's `joiner_before: Vec<Option<String>>`, where `None` is a hard newline and `Some(ws)` is a soft-wrap carrying the exact skipped whitespace), because only the algorithm that *creates* a break knows whether it was soft — and if copy has to guess, it copies code wrong.

### Cursive: the retained tree that lost mindshare anyway

Cursive is the existence proof that a retained tree works in Rust — but it works only by inventing a whole vocabulary of workarounds, each of which is a scar from the borrow checker. A view's `on_event(&mut self)` can never receive `&mut Cursive` (that would be mutable aliasing of a tree the app owns), so handlers return `EventResult::Consumed(Option<Callback>)` and the root runs the callback *after* the tree borrow ends. Stateful `FnMut` callbacks need the `immut1!/immut2!` macros, which wrap the closure in a `Mutex` and silently skip the call on reentrant lock. Holding a "reference" to a view outside the tree means an `ArcMutexGuard` that panics if another reference exists. Every one of these is the borrow checker's fingerprint pressed into the API.

The telling part is the ecosystem verdict. When `tui-rs` (Ratatui's ancestor) was dying, its own maintainer conceded retained mode's advantages — "It is hard to build complex UI abstractions on top of `tui`: scroll, mouse support and advanced layouts are… challenging" — and explicitly suggested porting `tui`'s look-and-feel onto Cursive and deprecating `tui` ([issue #654](https://github.com/fdehau/tui-rs/issues/654)). The community ignored the recommendation and built Ratatui — immediate mode — instead, because "we avoid a lot of lifetime and ownership issues common to retained mode UIs in Rust." Immediate mode won mindshare *against the losing framework's own maintainer's advice*, on the strength of dodging exactly the ownership fight Cursive's workaround stack exists to manage. That is a data point about Rust's grain, not about which architecture is theoretically superior.

### Textual: the cautionary success

Textual is the most complete existence proof that a browser-grade retained DOM + CSS + compositor works in the terminal — its compositor algorithm (cuts → chops → occlusion → merge, per line, with a spatial map for O(1) culling) is the best-documented damage-region design in any TUI framework, and its CSS-with-live-reload is the single most-envied feature in the whole field ([algorithms post](https://textual.textualize.io/blog/2024/12/12/algorithms-for-high-performance-terminal-apps/)). And yet: its flagship `DataTable` takes ~63 seconds to load a 538k-row dataset versus 0.077s for the community `textual-fastdatatable` fork — an ~800× gap, never fixed in core through v8 ([fastdatatable](https://github.com/tconbeer/textual-fastdatatable)). Textualize's own log viewer, Toolong, *bypasses the built-in scrollables* and ships a custom ScrollView because gigabyte files need virtualization the stock widgets don't offer. And it must be said plainly that Textual's celebrated "CSS" is a smaller thing than the name implies — layout is dock/linear/grid/`fr` with exact fractions, not flexbox and not a constraint solver — and that "fake CSS" uncanny valley drew real complaints ("They should have called it something else and used different syntax" — darkstar999, [HN 37174657](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=37174657)).

The company wound down in May 2025 — the framework was "mature and battle-tested" but they "struggled to identify a viable commercial problem" ([future of Textualize](https://textual.textualize.io/blog/2025/05/07/the-future-of-textualize/)) — and yet maintenance mode has not meant decay: v8.2.8 shipped 2026-06-30. But it is now a one-person show that shipped **eight major versions between 1.0 and 8.x in eighteen months**, and the community forks widgets rather than patching a monolithic core. Textual is simultaneously the best argument for the retained-DOM-plus-CSS model and the clearest warning about its maintenance economics.

## Part IV: What actually changed in the last two years

The single largest shift in the field is that **AI is now three things at once**: the dominant *workload*, a major *author population*, and — increasingly — a target *audience* for API design.

As a **workload**, agent CLIs are ~90% streaming rich text, and they impose requirements older frameworks never faced: inline scrollback (so users keep native scrolling, Cmd-F, and copy), streaming markdown that reflows correctly on resize, structured non-prose cells (diffs, tool logs, exec output), and modal approvals that interrupt a live stream. This is why inline rendering vaulted from "differentiating feature nobody has" to the sharpest demand of the era. Alt-screen-centric designs — the Ratatui family — structurally underserve it; Ink's [#359](https://github.com/vadimdemedes/ink/issues/359) has been open since 2020 precisely because its erase-and-rewrite model can't preserve scrollback. Every agent vendor keeps extracting the same in-house framework (`a3s-tui`, `capo-tui`, and others) because nothing off-the-shelf serves a streaming markdown transcript with a diff view and a tool log.

As an **author population**, AI changed the economics of ports. Before 2024, a faithful port of a beloved foreign framework "can't be finished part-time" — `intuitive`, a React-flavored Rust TUI, has a README that now reads "This crate currently does not work… It's unmaintained." In 2026, the AI wave produced faithful ports of *all five* envied foreign frameworks in months: Textual (`textual-rs`), Bubble Tea (`bubbletea-rs`, `charmed_rust`), Ink (`inkferro`), Rich (`rich_rust`, `richrs`), Turbo Vision (two revivals in a fortnight). The tax didn't vanish — it *moved*, from writing the port to verifying it. Which produces the era's most important shift in what constitutes a moat.

**Correctness is the new moat, because AI made widget breadth free.** The FrankenTUI reception is the whole thesis in one HN comment: "if you look visually it looks like it's working. Once I tried interacting with it, everything is broken in a subtle way" ([46986644](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=46986644)). When a language model can emit eighty widgets in a weekend, the widgets stop being the scarce good. The scarce good is *correct focus, mouse, resize, CJK width, and Windows key-handling* — and, crucially, the *verification harnesses* that prove it. The disciplined tail of the AI wave invented exactly this: `textual-rs` built a real-PTY cell-grid parity harness that diffs its output against a reference implementation (and documented the key failure — headless render tests *passed while the live app was broken*); `inkferro` runs a 22,500-comparison differential fuzz against a *live Node oracle*, byte-for-byte. The bar for trustworthy TUI development is now PTY-level, and it doubles as the trust mechanism for AI-assisted work. tui2's contribution here is the specific missing rung above buffer-equality: a `CrosstermBackend<vt100::Parser>` that drives real escape sequences through an actual terminal emulator and asserts on the resulting screen grid — the only way to catch the scroll-region and clear-code bugs that live at the ANSI level, not the buffer level.

## Part V: What is still genuinely contested

Not everything converged. Three questions remain open, and honest framework authors should treat them as forks, not settled.

**The programming model.** The evidence pulls in different directions depending on which domain you weight. The GUI literature favors the Xilem view/element split — an ephemeral typed view tree diffed each cycle against a retained widget core, with a single `&mut AppState` threaded down id-paths — on the strength of identity, incremental view computation, and accessibility. The terminal evidence is less sure: Textual's retained-tree-plus-reactive-attributes is the model *proven at real app scale*, but its Rust instances stall on adoption, not architecture (`rooibos`, five stars). Bubble Tea's Elm loop is the most *teachable* model in existence — three methods and `Cmd = func() Msg`, 18k+ dependents — and also the one that "collapses under composition," per six years of unresolved message-routing complaints. What the survey *does* settle is narrower but firm: a pure Elm monolith is ruled out (message routing to children is where TEA dies), and any surviving model needs framework-owned stable identity plus a designated home for retained state. Among the survivors — Xilem-style diffing, Textual-style retained reactivity, and immediate-mode-with-frame-facts — the survey cannot adjudicate. It is a live fork.

**Inline versus alt-screen viewport ownership.** tui2 proved this is a cliff, not a slope. The honest position is that a framework should offer *both* — terminal-native inline mode where the terminal keeps scrollback/selection/copy, and an app-owned viewport mode — and document loudly that the second obligates you to reimplement wheel physics, selection geometry, copy fidelity, and per-terminal escape quirks. The default should be native; the owned path is justified only when the workload (faithful code copy across reflow, rich per-cell interaction) genuinely demands it.

**Styling.** Three independent literal-CSS engines in one year say the *demand* is real; Textual says the *model* works; the Rust GUI world says the *mechanism* — "efficiently associate values of arbitrary types with any widget" — is a genuinely unsolved storage problem (Masonry named it its single biggest usability blocker). The unresolved question is whether a Rust TUI can deliver Textual-grade themeable, live-reloadable styling *without* Textual's retained DOM underneath it. Ink's ecosystem suggests a cheaper answer might suffice — semantic color tokens threaded through context, which is what both `ink-ui`'s `extendTheme` and Gemini CLI's settings.json themes converged on independently, with zero demand for selectors or a cascade. Nobody has proven which model the terminal actually needs.

## Part VI: What would actually move the field

If the last two years were about relearning what the cell grid already knew, the next two are about the problems nobody has solved.

**Accessibility is the open forcing-function.** In a fifty-framework wave, not one addressed assistive-technology semantics — "no standard to communicate TUI semantics to assistive technology." The GUI analysis is unambiguous that this is the thing that eliminates pure immediate mode for production, because screen readers assume a retained, stably-identified tree. There is an exploratory Ratatui discussion on an AccessKit-style export ([#2190](https://github.com/ratatui/ratatui/discussions/2190)), but it is a discussion, not a fact — even the GUI experts hedge that "you never know for sure until you actually try it." Whoever ships the first credible terminal accessibility export will have both a genuine differentiator and, quite possibly, the deciding argument in the programming-model fork above: if accessibility demands a persistent identified tree, the retained-core camp wins by default. This is the most likely future forcing-function on TUI architecture, and it is wide open.

**The substrate is quietly rotting out from under everyone, and someone should own the negotiation layer.** Terminfo is dead in practice: libvaxis states flatly that it "does not use terminfo — support for vt features is detected through terminal queries," and Helix ships a *hardcoded WezTerm check* for undercurl because "terminfo entries are wrong/stale" ([helix #13224](https://github.com/helix-editor/helix/pull/13224)). The replacement is query-based capability negotiation — libvaxis's batched query burst, fenced by a DA1 reply as an end-sentinel, with a timeout — and it is the single highest-value substrate pattern in the survey. But it is unfinished business: width/grapheme negotiation (mode 2027) is only partly deployed, so framework and terminal can disagree about how many cells an emoji occupies and desync the cursor on every frame; the incumbent Rust backend, crossterm, has a *chronic* query-race wound where `cursor::position()` fails under PTY forwarding and a lone `ESC` byte gets misread as a keypress because the parser depends on `read()` syscall chunking ([#963](https://github.com/crossterm-rs/crossterm/issues/963), [#993](https://github.com/crossterm-rs/crossterm/issues/993)) — two consumers of one byte stream with no router between them. And a large share of real sessions run inside tmux, where the honest capability answer changes per version (tmux 3.7 does modes 2026/2031 but swallows kitty-keyboard and 2027 entirely). A backend that gets negotiation, multiplexer provenance, and query/input routing right — the way Helix's `termina` and libvaxis do, and the way the mode 2048 in-band-resize spec (authored to replace "inherently racy" SIGWINCH) points — would remove a whole class of jank the entire field currently ships around.

**The winning move is already visible, and it is not architecture.** Every surviving framework in the survey is kept alive by exactly one real application, and every dead one died with its author's flagship app — `zi` died with the `zee` editor; `tui-realm` lives exactly as long as `termscp` needs it. The frameworks with traction layer incrementally on Ratatui's 36-million-download gravity rather than demanding wholesale buy-in; the ones that replace it forfeit the widget ecosystem and stall. The HN complaint list is a product spec hiding in plain sight: a batteries-included widget catalog (the everyday cases — spinners, checkboxes, text areas — not raw count), a blessed event loop, and inline mode, all consistently versioned and PTY-tested. The catalog *is* the product, which is why `rooibos` has five stars and Textual won an ecosystem with thirty-five documented widgets, live-reload CSS, and devtools. Architecture novelty is free now. Correct interaction behavior, a real widget catalog, a headless test harness that an AI author can run, and one serious reference app treated as an acceptance test — those are the scarce goods.

## Coda

The terminal is fifty years old and, in 2026, more contested than it has been since the VT100. But the contest is not the one it looks like. It looks like fifty frameworks fighting over a programming model. It is really the whole field — Go conceding string diffing, JavaScript relearning the alt-screen buffer, Rust rediscovering that immediate mode fights identity, a coding agent proving that owning the viewport is a cliff — arriving, from every direction at once, at the same short list of things a terminal UI must do and the same shorter list of things it must not bother doing. The demand survey has been run, fifty times over, by humans and language models alike, and the answer came back identical every time. What is missing is not another architecture. It is someone willing to build the boring, correct, well-documented, thoroughly-tested middle — and to keep a real application alive on top of it for longer than one person's attention span. That is a harder problem than any renderer. It is also the only one left that matters.
